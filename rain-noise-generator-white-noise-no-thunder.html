Rain vs. White Noise vs. Pink Noise

Traditional white noise sounds like static — equal energy at every frequency, harsh on the ears. Pink noise rolls off the highs and is gentler, closer to what nature actually sounds like. Rain noise falls in the pink-noise zone but adds organic, irregular droplet textures the brain interprets as "real" and safe.

That's why rain noise outperforms both: as effective as white noise at masking, as pleasant as pink noise to listen to, and more natural than either.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a rain noise generator?expand_more

A tool or recording that produces a continuous, flat rain sound for use as a noise mask — like white noise, but more natural and pleasant.

Is rain noise the same as white noise?expand_more

Not exactly. Rain noise sits closer to pink noise — gentler highs than white noise — and is more pleasant to listen to for long stretches.

Does rain noise help with tinnitus?expand_more

Many tinnitus sufferers use steady rain as a mask. A flat curve (no thunder peaks) works best because it doesn't draw attention back to the silence between sounds.

Is a procedurally generated rain better than a recording?expand_more

For long listening sessions, yes — procedural rain never repeats, so the brain can't lock onto a pattern. Recordings always loop eventually, and the seam is detectable.
